## May 21, 2025

**Headline:** New Senate opens term with rules vote and finance extension  
**Slug:** `new-senate-opens-with-rules-vote-and-finance-extension`  
**Dek:** The 76th ASUCSB Senate used its first meeting to adopt standing rules, keep finance operations running, and begin the transition into a new term.

The first meeting of the 76th ASUCSB Senate was more procedural than dramatic, but it still set the tone for the months ahead. Senators adopted the new body’s standing rules and approved a motion extending the term of members of the 75th Senate Finance Committee through the end of Spring 2025. The stated goal was to keep financial operations moving while the new Senate got itself organized.

The meeting also included the first round of appointments and a short transition message from President Le Anh Metzger, who said her office was beginning transition meetings and would soon send out information on incoming board appointments. For students looking for sweeping policy action, this was not that meeting. But for anyone tracking how AS actually works, the opening session mattered: before the Senate could debate student programs, it first had to define its own rules and keep the money pipeline functioning.

## May 28, 2025

**Headline:** Senate’s second meeting turns into a fight over transparency, judicial rulings, and retroactive fixes  
**Slug:** `senate-fight-over-transparency-judicial-rulings-and-retroactive-fixes`  
**Dek:** A packed May 28 meeting mixed presentations on student government finances with disputes over Judicial Council directives, quorum, and retroactive action.

The May 28 Senate meeting was one of the first signs that the 76th Senate would spend as much time debating process as policy. Senators took up resolutions on internal controls and compliance, the FARO Center, and the Human Rights Board, but the larger story was institutional confusion. Speakers debated how to comply with a Judicial Council directive involving a prior appointment, whether the Senate should reopen old minutes, and what kind of retroactive correction was legally defensible.

A separate thread ran through the meeting: public frustration with Associated Students’ financial transparency. One speaker described difficulty accessing expenditure data and argued that even students who pay AS fees have little idea how the organization works. The meeting closed with more procedural tension, including concern over quorum and the practical question of whether the Senate could keep conducting business under disputed conditions. It was an early glimpse of a body already wrestling with the limits of its own systems.

## June 4, 2025

**Headline:** Senate approves retroactive actions as legal confusion deepens  
**Slug:** `senate-approves-retroactive-actions-as-legal-confusion-deepens`  
**Dek:** A marathon June 4 meeting mixed conflict-of-interest guidance, retroactive funding, Judicial Council fallout, and questions about travel and liability.

The June 4 Senate meeting was one of the most revealing sessions of the year. Senators approved or debated a series of retroactive actions, including funding fixes, reimbursement-related motions, and corrections tied to earlier Senate decisions. At several points, senators openly questioned whether the Senate should be doing so much business retroactively at all.

The meeting also featured an extended address from the Attorney General’s office on conflict of interest, honoraria errors, and a recent Judicial Council ruling. Senators were told to think carefully before voting on matters that could benefit friends, associates, or organizations with which they were closely tied. Later in the meeting, discussion turned to international travel and whether Senate or Finance Committee exceptions could legally cover it without administrative approval. The result was a picture of a student government trying to govern while also repairing prior procedural damage in real time.

The Senate’s November 12 session combined almost every major theme of the year into one meeting. If you want one meeting that shows why students should pay attention to Senate procedure, this is it.

## January 28, 2026

**Headline:** BCU spending audit gives Senate its clearest look yet at internal versus student-facing spending  
**Slug:** `bcu-spending-audit-gives-senate-its-clearest-look-yet-at-spending`  
**Dek:** A new January 28 presentation on honoraria, rollover, and internal spending may become one of the most important reference points in this year’s budget fights.

The January 28 Senate meeting featured one of the most consequential presentations of the year: a report analyzing BCU budgets, honoraria levels, rollover balances, and how much money appears to go inward toward internal operations rather than directly back to students. Presenters were careful not to call it an indictment, but senators openly described the results as useful for upcoming budget-cycle decisions and possible structural reform.

Just as importantly, staff warned that the numbers are still incomplete because fiscal-year closeout remains unfinished and the transition to a new requisitions system has created backlog and uncertainty. The Senate also considered election-code changes, transparency and accountability policy revisions, an antisemitism resolution, and another committee allocation request. If December 3 exposed the problem, January 28 may be the meeting that finally gave senators the data language to start acting on it.
