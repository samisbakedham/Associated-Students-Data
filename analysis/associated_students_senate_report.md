# Associated Students Senate Report

## Scope

This report reviews the Associated Students UCSB Senate agendas and minutes in this workspace from **May 21, 2025** through **January 28, 2026**. The corpus covers **23 dated records**: regular Senate meetings, one May email vote sequence, and one late-November email vote sequence. One standard agenda appears to be missing for **December 3, 2025**.

The review is based on the documents alone. It does **not** include bank records, ledgers, invoices, contracts, reimbursement logs, audio/video recordings, or outside legal review. Because of that, this report can identify **red flags, inconsistencies, and governance risks**, but it cannot responsibly conclude that anyone committed embezzlement, fraud, or another crime unless the minutes themselves clearly prove it. They do not.

## Executive Summary

The dominant story in this Senate is not a single scandal. It is a prolonged struggle over **how student government is run, who controls money, what counts as a valid appointment or vote, and how transparent the association really is**.

Across the period reviewed, the Senate repeatedly dealt with:

- retroactive approvals and corrections to earlier actions
- disputes over Judicial Council directives and Attorney General advice
- repeated conflict-of-interest warnings
- funding requests brought forward without complete budget detail
- closed sessions tied to finance minutes, spending, staffing, and student fees
- unresolved confusion over committee appointments and honoraria
- open admissions that financial data was incomplete, backlogged, or hard to verify

At the same time, the Senate was not inactive. It moved money to student programs, debated food insecurity responses, considered language revitalization and peer-mentor efforts, modernized some internal policies, created temporary committees, and pushed reforms around honoraria and finance visibility.

The most defensible conclusion is this:

**The documents show serious governance and transparency problems, but they do not by themselves prove theft, kickbacks, or embezzlement.**

## What Actually Happened

### 1. Spring and early summer were dominated by procedural repair work

The opening meetings of the 76th Senate focused on standing rules, transition, and continuing finance operations. Within one week, the tone shifted into heavy procedural conflict.

By **May 28, 2025** and **June 4, 2025**, the Senate was wrestling with:

- how to respond to a Judicial Council directive
- whether and how to reopen or correct prior minutes
- whether retroactive appointments were lawful
- whether retroactive funding could be approved
- how to handle conflict-of-interest questions

These meetings matter because they set the pattern for the year: internal rules fights were not side issues, they were central business.

### 2. Summer meetings mixed policy work with governance cleanup

The summer meetings pushed some legislation forward, including updates to BCU election rules, Pearman Fellowship language, and Office of the President code, while also continuing to clean up earlier governance problems.

The Senate also handled:

- retroactive funding exceptions
- an advisory-opinion request related to legality
- IV Arts funding questions
- committee leadership and appointment disputes

This period already showed a recurring weakness: when regular committee pipelines lagged or failed, the Senate often compensated with **exceptions, retroactive fixes, or floor improvisation**.

### 3. Fall became a battle over amendments, budget visibility, and finance process

From **October through November 2025**, multiple meetings show senators objecting that:

- legislation came back from committee without clearly marked amendments
- itemized budgets were missing or late
- email votes and committee minutes were not easy to audit
- “minutes and allocations” could be approved in bulk without meaningful review

The Senate also spent substantial time on:

- finance committee structure
- honoraria policy
- committee membership changes
- executive director search mechanics
- Senate committee budgets
- funding for basic-needs and food-support programs

The fall records show an institution trying to reform itself while still operating through the same weak systems it was criticizing.

### 4. December and January made the transparency crisis explicit

The clearest single transparency warning appears in **December 2025** and **January 2026**.

On **December 3, 2025**, speakers described the existing minutes and finance workflow as error-prone, opaque, and outdated. They argued that students and even insiders often lacked a reliable “ground truth” about who held positions, what had been approved, and what money had actually been spent.

On **January 28, 2026**, a spending-analysis presentation went further. Speakers described a report on:

- how much BCU money goes to honoraria
- how much is internally focused versus visibly student-facing
- where rollover money is accumulating
- how incomplete records and a requisitions backlog make precise judgment difficult

Financial operations staff also warned that prior-year numbers were still incomplete because the fiscal year had not fully closed and the system transition had created a backlog. That is not proof of wrongdoing. It is proof of weak financial visibility.

## Major Themes

### Governance and legal procedure

The Senate repeatedly debated:

- standing rules versus legal code
- when appointments expire
- who has authority to interpret ambiguous procedure
- when to seek Judicial Council advisory opinions
- whether Senate actions should be tabled, reopened, or corrected

The pattern suggests a body spending major political energy on **institutional self-definition** rather than just policy outcomes.

### Money and budget politics

Funding decisions came up constantly. The Senate discussed or approved support for:

- Arab Student Group
- Black Heritage Cultural initiatives
- Moot Court
- rent support
- grocery vouchers
- food programs
- Speech Forensics
- Living History Project
- weekly Senate dinners
- Senate committee project budgets
- Attorney General honoraria
- Office of the President additional honoraria and project funding

At several points senators complained that requests arrived without enough documentation, without multiple quotes, or without clearly itemized budgets.

### Honoraria and internal compensation

Honoraria was one of the most persistent fault lines. The Senate debated:

- retroactive honoraria
- committee and BCU honoraria levels
- whether new positions increased internal spending too far
- modernization of honoraria process
- whether some bodies were spending too much of their budgets on themselves

This is a legitimate accountability issue. It is not inherently corruption. But it becomes a red flag when combined with poor reporting, unclear appointments, and incomplete books.

### Transparency and controls

Several meetings called for:

- stronger controls and compliance structures
- better minutes systems
- cleaner appointment tracking
- easier access to budget and expenditure data
- more auditable approvals

When the institution itself is repeatedly saying that its records are hard to verify, that is one of the strongest reporting leads in the entire archive.

## Red-Flag Assessment

### What the documents do **not** prove

The records reviewed do **not** provide direct proof of:

- embezzlement
- theft
- kickbacks
- fake vendors
- falsified payments
- personal diversion of funds into private accounts

There are many arguments about money, but no clear documentary smoking gun for criminal financial misconduct in this set alone.

### What the documents **do** show

#### 1. Retroactive approvals were common enough to be a systemic concern

Multiple meetings in late spring and summer dealt with retroactive funding, retroactive appointments, retroactive corrections to prior minutes, and retroactive recognition of prior actions. That is risky because it weakens the normal rule that public bodies should act prospectively, with clear records and review before money or power moves.

Key dates:

- **May 28, 2025**
- **May 31, 2025** email vote
- **June 4, 2025**
- **July 9, 2025**
- **January 7, 2026** for retroactive appointments and resignations

#### 2. Financial visibility was openly described as inadequate

This is one of the strongest documented concerns in the archive.

By **December 3, 2025**, speakers were explicitly arguing that the current minutes and finance system made it too hard to know what had actually happened. By **January 28, 2026**, staff and presenters acknowledged that backlog and fiscal-close issues meant some figures were incomplete and student groups did not always have reliable current expenditure data.

That does not mean money was stolen. It does mean the system was weak enough that misuse could be harder to catch.

#### 3. Itemized budget review was inconsistent

Repeated comments show senators asking for:

- itemized budgets
- multiple quotes
- clearer funding sources
- better public documentation before votes

These complaints come up in **October 1**, **November 5**, **November 12**, **December 3**, and **January 7**. If you are looking for a credible accountability angle, this is stronger than a vague corruption accusation: the Senate often appears to have operated with **insufficient pre-vote documentation**.

#### 4. Conflict-of-interest warnings were frequent

The Office of the Attorney General repeatedly raised conflict-of-interest issues, especially around appointments, honoraria, and votes that could materially affect colleagues or associates.

Key dates:

- **June 4, 2025**
- **October 1, 2025**
- **October 15, 2025**
- **November 12, 2025**
- **December 3, 2025**
- **January 7, 2026**

This does not prove conflicts were improperly hidden in every case. It does show the body repeatedly operating close enough to the line that its own legal office had to keep warning people.

#### 5. Bulk approval of “minutes and allocations” looks like a structural weakness

Again and again, the Senate bundled large sets of minutes and allocations together. In **November 12, 2025**, senators openly criticized the system by noting that material could be dropped into the approval folder and passed before anyone had actually reviewed it carefully.

That is a governance risk. It may be the single best “process corruption” story in the archive, meaning not bribery, but a system that makes shallow approval too easy.

#### 6. Closed-session usage deserves outside scrutiny

The Senate repeatedly entered closed session on matters involving:

- finance committee minutes
- student fees
- IV Arts funds and staff reorganization
- BCU structuring and association spending

The body repeatedly cited the **Brown Act** when entering or describing these sessions. Based on the minutes alone, I cannot determine whether those sessions complied with every procedural requirement. But the pattern is notable enough that a reporter or watchdog should review:

- agendas for closed-session notice language
- who was admitted
- what topics were actually discussed
- what actions, if any, followed in open session

Key dates:

- **October 8, 2025**
- **October 29, 2025**
- **November 5, 2025**
- **December 3, 2025**
- **January 14, 2026**

#### 7. Budget overruns and midyear add-on requests merit scrutiny

The cleanest example is **January 21, 2026**, when the President presented a request for:

- **$11,300** in additional honoraria
- **$32,000** in additional special-project funding

after reporting that the office had already nearly exhausted its existing budget. That is not illegal. It is a real accountability question, especially given the Senate’s own complaints about internal spending and honoraria growth.

Other examples include repeated requests for additional committee or office money from unallocated funds during the year.

## Most Newsworthy Meetings

If the goal is public awareness, these are the strongest meetings to foreground:

### May 28, 2025

Big transition meeting. Judicial Council issues, retroactive corrections, financial transparency arguments, and public frustration all appear early here.

### June 4, 2025

Probably the most important spring procedural meeting. Conflict-of-interest guidance, retroactive funding, travel-liability questions, and finance/controls disputes all show up here.

### October 8, 2025

Closed session over finance committee minutes and Brown Act language. Strong transparency angle.

### November 5, 2025

Basic-needs funding meets process concerns. Useful example of a sympathetic policy goal running into documentation and procedure problems.

### November 12, 2025

One of the best meetings for showing the Senate’s internal critique of itself: honoraria concerns, committee allocations, advisory-opinion confusion, and criticism of the “minutes and allocations” system.

### December 3, 2025

Possibly the single most important meeting in the archive for financial-governance reporting. The minutes openly describe a broken transparency system, incomplete financial visibility, controversy over internal appointments, and more funding fights.

### January 21, 2026

Strong accountability meeting because the President’s office requests large additional funds while broader financial strain remains a theme.

### January 28, 2026

Important because the BCU spending analysis formalizes what had previously been an argument: how much money is going inward, how much is reaching students, and how incomplete the numbers still are.

## Bottom Line

There is enough here for aggressive reporting, but the strongest version of that reporting is **not** “the minutes prove embezzlement.”

The stronger and better-supported claim is:

**The minutes show a student government with weak controls, incomplete financial visibility, recurring procedural confusion, and repeated disputes over whether money and appointments are being handled cleanly.**

That is a serious public-interest story on its own.

## Best Next Steps

If you want to push this beyond a document review, the next evidence to gather is:

1. Full expenditure reports for Senate, BCUs, and the major offices requesting extra funds.
2. Reimbursement and requisition logs, especially where students said they paid out of pocket or waited months.
3. Closed-session agenda language and any open-session follow-up actions.
4. The “to be approved” minutes-and-allocations folders as they existed before votes.
5. Conflict-of-interest forms filed with the Attorney General or executive office.
6. Budget-cycle spreadsheets, rollover sheets, and honoraria breakdowns referenced in the meetings.
7. Any CPRA responses or internal financial dashboards referenced by senators or staff.
